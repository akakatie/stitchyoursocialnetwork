Stitch Your Social Network


Facebook collects large volumes of personal data, from searches, to messages and connections across your entire social network. Stitch Your Social Network is a project that offers a way to reclaim your data in a meaningful and reflective way.

The project involves embroidering personal annotations on top of a generated pattern based on your facebook friends. It provides the opportunity to reflect on how digital connections reflect or don't reflect real life experiences. Embroidery is a contemplative craft, giving you time to not only explore, but speak back to your data with your own stitches and connections.

The project is open to anyone looking to take back their data ownership, or just have some fun with embroidery. We’ve compiled some steps below to get started!




Step 1: Gathering the Data and Generating the Pattern

We've put together an R notebook that steps you through how to download your facebook data and generate a base pattern for your embroidery.

  • View the notebook preview

  • Download the .Rmd

Base pattern

The base pattern is a circle of dots, with each dot representing a facebook friend. The dots are arranged clockwise in the order you connected with the friends. Dots are drawn closer to the centre of the circle depending on how frequently and recently that friend has messaged you. The dots on the very edge of the circle are facebook friends that have never messaged you. You can control the size of the circle and the distance of the dots in the R Script.




Step 2: Transferring the Pattern

Once you’ve got your pattern and some embroidery materials (an embroidery hoop, some fabric, a needle, some thread), it's time to transfer the data onto the fabric. There are many ways patterns can be transferred. Here we detail the options that worked well for us:

Fabric Print Method:

1. Cut a piece of freezer paper to the size of an A4 page.




2. Put it shiny side down on your fabric and iron lightly until it sticks.




3. Cut out the reinforced section of fabric. Load into an inkjet printer. Print


Transfer Paper Method:

This method is particularly useful if you’re using dark fabric that's hard to print on. The YouTube channel Lolli and Grace provides a useful tutorial on how to do this. We’ve also made a list of the steps you can follow.

Lolli and Grace video resource: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7U4t7DEWBYI

1. Print out your PDF data pattern to the same size as your embroidery hoop

2. Secure your fabric in the hoop and place with the fabric against a flat surface.

3. Cut a piece of white transfer paper and your pattern to the size of your embroidery hoop and tape inside.




4. Use a pen to firmly trace all the dots, this will transfer them onto the fabric.




5. Remove the paper and go over the dots in a white gel pen to make the more visible.




Tip: If your pattern isn’t too complicated you can also try taping the pattern to the paper and drawing the dots by poking through the paper with a white gel pen!



Step 3: Embroidery

Once you’ve transferred your pattern it's time to start stitching! If you’re new to embroidery, here are a couple of resources we found helpful for getting started:

Some basic beginner stitches: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OWv6Ypzn9dg
Some more complicated beginner stitches: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sib-U2Np7G8

Stuck on what to stitch? Everyone’s approach to their embroidery will be different, but we’ve listed a some questions we found helpful to prompt our own processes:
Are the people who are important to me represented as important in the data?
Are they even represented at all?
How might I explore or show different groups of people in my life?
Do I want to stitch all the data points, or only a few?

There’s no right or wrong way to go about it! We also found it helpful to draw some plans onto pattern paper printouts with markers.




Step 4: Share

If you like, you can go full circle and share your social network on social media using #stitchyoursocialnetwork!
